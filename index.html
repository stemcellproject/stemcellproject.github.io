Hi Everyone!

Our team consists of Taylor, Noel, Brian and Annie. We created a website to clarify some misunderstandings about stem cells and stem cell research. In class, there was quite a bit of confusion about the potencies of different stem cells, so we have a section of our website dedicated to explaining the difference between each potency. We also found that many people believe they are "against stem cell research," but often people saying that are only thinking about embryonic stem cells. To clarify what stem cells research includes, we created a section detailing what types of stem cells there are, where they come from, and what current or potential uses they have.

Our website is interactive, to go through the website scroll down! There will be some points where you can click to find more information and beautiful artwork, so make sure to look out for the "Click" instructions!

1868: The term "stem cell" first appears in scientific literature.
1909: Alexander Maximow theorizes that all blood cells come from the same ancestor cell.
1953: Leroy Stevens finds large tumors in mice's scrotums.
1957: E. Donnall Thomas attempts the first human bone marrow transplantation.
1968: Robert A. Good performs the first successful bone marrow transplant
1986: Scientists take connective tissue cells and turn them into muscle cells
1997: Dominique Bonnet and John Dick discover that leukemia comes from the same stem cells that make our blood cells
1998: A team at the University of Wisconsin reports the creation of the first batch of human embryonic stem cells
2001: President George W. Bush signs an order authorizing the use of federal funds for research on a limited number of existing human embryonic stem cell lines
2006: Shinya Yamanaka and Kazutoshi Takahashi announce the creation of rodent induced pluripotent cells
2009: President Barack Obama signs Executive Order 13505 to repeal some of the restrictions on human embryonic stem cell research funds
2009: The NIH issues the revised guidelines on federal funding for stem cell research

Totipotent

stem cells can form any cell type in the body or placenta

The 1st totipotent cell forms when the sperm and egg unite.

They continue being formed until the 4th day.

Then no more totipotent cells are made, but there are three other potencies!

Pluripotent

stem cells can form any cell type that makes up the body

Embryonic cells are an example of pluripotent cells.

They can become any type of cell, other than totipotent cells.

For example, they can become hair cells, or blood cells, or skin cells, or brain cells!

Multipotent

stem cells can form into a specific number of types of cells.

Multipotent cells are similar to pluripotent stem cells.

However, each multipotent cell cannot turn into all other cells.

They can only turn into a select number of other cell types.

Unipotent

stem cells can only form into one type of cell

Unipotent stem cells can only turn into one type of cell.

Examples of unipotent cells are skin cells!

Embryonic

Embryonic stem cells are taken from the blastocyst, which occurs in an early developmental stage in embryonic development.

Embryonic stem cells are not currently in use in any way.

Their potential uses include:

  1. Organ generation
  2. Safe testing of drugs

Adult

Adult stem cells can be found in many places including bone marrow, the brain, blood, and the intestine.

An example of their use:

  1. Bone marrow transplantations!
  2. Growing skin for burn victims to help them recover and heal.
  3. A potential use is organ generation.

Adult stem cells are naturally unipotent, but can be induced to become pluripotent.

Induced Pluripotent

These are unipotent cells that are manipulated to become pluripotent.

Their potential uses are the same as that of embryonic stem cells.
